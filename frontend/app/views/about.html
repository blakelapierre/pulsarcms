Programming

In 4th grade, I began formally studying the BASIC programming language in my school's Gifted and Talented Education (GATE) program. By 7th grade, I had a video game entered into a state-wide competition. Between my junior and senior years of high school, I was selected as one of two students to attend Carnegie Mellon's Advanced Placement/Early Admission program where I studied the Pascal programming language in a University setting.

I've formally studied BASIC, Pascal, C, Assembly and COBOL (in that exact order). I've taught myself C++, Java, JavaScript, Lua, TCL, CSS and HTML. I have written full TCP/IP stacks that run in user space with their own ARP modules, etc., to simulate millions of "virtual" PCs using only 96 rack-mounted hosts. And, I've written systems that punch through consumer-grade NAT hardware to connect game players in peer-to-peer grids on PCs and video game consoles.

I used to run the OnDemand! BBS as part of the networks that predate the Internet and ran entirely on dial-up modems. I've built BBS "door" games in C on DOS. I've built systems that test very high capacity Cisco switches and routers. I've built the systems that power some of your favorite online games. And, I've built systems that simulate a full range of medical conditions experienced by a soldier at war in Iraq at Texas A&M University-Corpus Christi. I've written a smoothed particle hydrodynamics system to simulate human blood in real time as a nurse stresses over which decisions are the right decisions.

Try to understand: I've built systems that have made battle-hardened nurses who have served in actaul war...faint. I am very specifically talking about my code running on an insanely fast Alienware laptop on a series of projectors at an actual military base in south Texas. At that presentation, an actual military nurse...fainted. And, that's because when you ask me to, "Keep it real," you seriously have no idea what kind of twisted shit is headed your way. Please, think carefully before asking. Because they said, "It absolutely has to be as close to real life as possible."

I built a shader that simulates the oxidation of blood over time taking it from a smooth, shiny liquid through to full clot (hard, crusty, cracks). The particle system considered coagulation and ambient environment temperatures as variables. The simulation was implemented against Ageia Phys-X with a continuous time domain and a smoothed particle hydrodynamics simulation that responded to the rigid bodies in the scene. I was, therefore, able to also have those algorithms consider the effects of surface friction and the properties of the blood to make the blood "stick" to surfaces and "slide" and "slosh" around as if it existed in reality. The simulation was precise and tested correct. It would stain clothing differently than painted surfaces differently than sand, for example.

It made a military nurse faint. I'm done talking.
