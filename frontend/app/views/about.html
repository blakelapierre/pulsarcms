I am a veteran software developer with over 20 years in games, extremely low-level networking, systems and embedded programming. I've written TCP/IP stacks from scratch in C++ that ran in user space on RHEL5 directly talking to the NIC with its own ARP module and more, memory heap managers for PlayStation®, and 2D/3D graphics, presentation frameworks and audio processing for anything with a CPU and audio chip from the phone in your pocket to very advanced training simulations built for the Office of Naval Research that runs on actual Naval submarines.

I am a full-stack Web developer who's done things like hack the TIME_WAIT state out of spent sockets in a custom Linux-based distributed test harness and framework and generally done whatever it takes to make things work and get work done. And, when doing that, I prefer to use the right tools for the job.

True, MEAN may not be the perfect tool for every job. But, I argue it is perfectly acceptable for use when I say it is and when I'm the one building the requested solution instead of you. You...get to learn and host it when it's done. And, if you're saying anything other than asking me how, I'm going to let you figure it out on your own...or quit. I do not care which.

After more than a decade of fighting with various "IT Professionals" over this & that, I decided to stop trying to succeed along that path and changed the questions I was asking.

I've stopped asking, "How do I show them this is the best way?" Today, I'm only asking this:

Why is this always a fight? Am I perhaps the one who is flawed in my constant pursuit of progress and innovation?

With this new mantra in mind, I set off on a journey to find the answer. Pulsar is that journey. And, it's a personal journey in open source community membership, modern technology stacks and personal patience. Because today, the page you're reading is being served by Pulsar, an application of my own creation sitting atop the MEAN stack.

And, it's running in production without issues. Ever. So, to all the ridiculousness that was, "That'll never work/never happen!" I simply offer: "Here it is. How's yesterday treatin' ya?"
